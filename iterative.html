Sesame Care: Iterative Design

Part 1: Sketches and Wireframe

Project Overview

This project was a group project for the course CSCI 1300: Interaction Design. The goal of the project was to create a design website for a startup company using an iterative design process. I, together with Sofia Guarisma, Kaley Newlin, and Valentina Lin, created a new website design for the company Sesame. Sesame is a healthcare company that provides affordable and accessible healthcare to everyone.

Sketching our Ideas

Our first step in working with Sesame was to understand what problems they wanted to tackle with our new design, as well as to brainstorm. The main points we had to tackle were:

  • Creating personalized and engaging webpages
  • Provide the user with enhanced understanding and personalized learning
  • Accessibility and clarity, making sure information is easy to understand and the website is easy to navigate.

We then each sketched out what we envisioned the product to be.

Part 2: Critiques and Changes

Design Choices

We then met with Vanessa Cho, the VP of Google Design Platforms. She gave us several suggestions on what to focus on and possible improvements to our design. We also got plenty of feedback from Ravi Mehta, Sesame's CPO, as well as their Lead Product Designer, Chris Chon.

Some of the main feedback was:

  • Order of website should be clear through visual hierarchy
  • Remove unnecessary components from screen
  • Clarify what questions each page is asking the user
  • Make less important components less prominent
  • Functionality of symptoms table unclear
  • Body diagram unclear

Conclusion

Future Changes

In the future, some changes we would like to make include:

  • Make scale of web components more standard
  • Avoid unclear wording
  • Break the Symptoms Page into multiple single-question windows
  • Change the first page of the Symptoms Page to ask if the pain is localized

Takeaways

My main takeaway from this project is how to make websites, especially those using AI features, more accessible and user-friendly. Through the numerous critiques we received, I learned how to take feedback and iterate on my designs. I also learned to focus on call to action and visual hierarchy to guide the users on how to use the application and what information is most important. Additionally, this group project taught me how to incorporate different ideas into a single design. Through this project, I deepened my understanding of how to design user-friendly, accessible websites, particularly those integrating AI-driven features. I learned how to iterate quickly based on feedback, refine designs to highlight key actions and information, and make applications easier for users to navigate and understand. This experience also strengthened my ability to merge diverse ideas into a cohesive design. All these skills are important when designing AI-based technology, because we must ensure that the technology is accessible to everyone.

I would like to thank Ravi Mehta, Chris Chon, Vanessa Cho, and Caroline Walters for their time and feedback. I would also like to thank the CSCI 1300 staff for their hard work and dedication.
