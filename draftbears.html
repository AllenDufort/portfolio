DraftBears Sportsbook

Overview

Project Description

This project analyzed NBA player performance metrics to uncover trends and correlations that can improve sports analytics. By examining data from different times of the season, we provide insights into how player performance impacts team success.

Context

This project was created by Melvin He, Jos√© Guallpa, and I as a final project for the course CSCI 1951A: Data Science at Brown University in Spring 2024. We were inspired to do this project because we were all fans of the NBA and we wanted to learn how to use data science in sports analytics.

Important Terms

  • Plus-minus score: difference between points scored by player's team and opposing team while player on court
  • Field goal (FG): basket made from field (not a free throw)
  • Field goal percentage (FG%): (FG made)/(FG attempted)
  • Defensive rebounds (DRB): number of rebounds player gets while defending against opposing team
  • Personal fouls (PF): number of fouls committed by player

Research Process

First, my group and I had to decide what we wanted to analyze. We wanted to find a dataset that included player performance metrics and team information, so we could analyze how player performance impacts team success. Then, we had to develop the research question that would guide our analysis. We decided to focus on the following question: "How do player performance metrics impact team success?" We then broke this question down into 3 hypotheses that we could test with our data:

  1. The higher the plus-minus score, the better the player performs in terms of scoring efficiency (higher FG%)
  2. There is no significant difference in the FG% of players in March compared to their average FG% over the season
  3. There is no significant correlation between the number of DRBs and PFs committed by players.

A major challenge was trying to find a dataset that included both player performance metrics and team information. My team and I spent a lot of time finding datasets that had one or both of these features. We ultimately collected 2 datasets from multiple APIs and then combined them into one database that included all of the player data we needed. Our database included various player performance metrics such as points per game, FG%, DRBs, and PFs.

After collecting the data, we had to clean it up and prepare it for analysis. We removed any records of players with missing or null values to ensure data integrity and completeness. We then ran multiple statistical tests on our data to assess our hypotheses.

Findings

Hypothesis 1: Accepted
Strong positive correlation found between plus-minus scores and FG%

  • Interpretation: We think that this is the case because if someone has a higher field goal percentage, they are likely to score more points which would make their plus-minus score higher

Hypothesis 2: Accepted
No significant difference found in players' FG% in March vs entire season

  • Interpretation: We theorize this may be the case due to continuous training, coaching consistency, and player experience

Hypothesis 3: Rejected
Positive correlation found between DRB and PF

  • Interpretation: We think this link exists because players who are more aggressive on defense are more likely to get rebounds and more likely to commit fouls

Conclusion

Takeaways

This project deepened my understanding of how complex and nuanced data analysis can be. For example, the correlation between plus-minus scores and FG% was lower than expected, revealing that many other factors, such as steals and rebounds, influence player performance metrics. I also learned that even with straightforward datasets, it's important to avoid overgeneralizing trends. Overall, this project reinforced the importance of mindful and cautious analysis as I build AI and ML models in order to accurately reflect the real-world data.

Ethical Considerations

Another key takeaway was the importance of considering ethical implications in data analysis. In sports analytics, racial bias remains a serious concern; for example, research by Foy and Roy (2023) highlighted how lighter-skinned players are often praised for cognitive abilities, while darker-skinned players are described more by physical traits. Although our dataset lacked qualitative descriptions, we took care to mitigate bias by avoiding analysis that linked physical attributes to race. As I continue working with AI and ML, maintaining ethical standards will be crucial to creating fair, inclusive, and trustworthy systems.
