Responsive Redesign

Overview

Have you ever ordered food online and found the website difficult to navigate? That was the experience I had when I tried ordered from Heng Thai and Rotisserie (or Heng for short).

This project is a responsive redesign of Heng's website, as part of the Interaction Design course at Brown University. I chose to analyze and redesign this website because Heng is my favorite restaurant in Providence, RI, and I believe their website can be improved.

Problems with Original Design

Going on Heng's home page, I always have trouble trying to figure out how to use the website. In order to improve the website, I wrote down all the issues that I found relating to usability and accessability.

Usability

Usability is basically how easy it is for someone to use an interface, like a website. For Heng, I noticed a few usability issues in terms of efficiency, learnability, and memorability.

  • Learnability:
    • The function of the 3 circles on the right side is unclear
    • Unclear where to click to order at first glance
  • Memorability:
    • Hard to remember what tabs are under "More..."
    • Hard to remember where "Order" and "DoorDash" buttons are
  • Efficiency:
    • The user must scroll all the way down to find restaurant address

Accessibility

WebAIM is an online resource to analyze the accessibility of websites. Using WebAIM's analysis tools, I found a few accessibility issues with the website, which are listed below:

  • Low contrast between text and background
  • Low contrast between buttons and button text
  • Missing alt text for some web elements

Overall, I agree with the WebAIM analysis on the website's accessibility. However, there are 2 other points of inaccessibility that I found that WebAIM didn't mention:

  • 3 circles on the right-hand side have low contrast with the black background
  • Text too small to read on mobile devices

Visual Redesign

In order to improve the usability of Heng's website, I decided to redesign the home page. In particular, my redesign addresses the accessability, learnability, efficiency, and memorability of the website. I made a visual design style guide and mockups on a desktop, tablets, and mobile device to illustrate the changes I made to the home page.

Updates

Here is a brief summary of the updates I made to the home page:

  • Layout reorganized to make website more intuitive and user-friendly
  • Increased font size in navigation bar and body text for better readability
  • Increased contrast in buttons and text body for better readability
  • Buttons moved to navigation bar for better efficiency
  • Navigation bar collapses on mobile devices and tablets
  • Logo added to the navigation bar to make it more visually appealing
  • Carousel added to make the website more visually appealing and to highlight specials
  • Buttons change color on hover for better visibility
  • Tabs underlined on hover for better visibility
  • Active tab underlined and bold for better visibility

Conclusion

This project was a great opportunity for me to practice the principles of usability and accessibility on a website. I learned about the many factors that go into responsive redesign, such as ensuring that the website works well on different devices and is user-friendly for all users, including those with disabilities. Analyzing Heng's website taught me how to identify and address usability and accessibility issues. This is especially important for me as a data scientist, as I want to create interfaces that are accessible to all users and provide a positive user experience.

I also developed skills in creating visual design style guides and mockups to communicate design ideas effectively. A strong understanding of design principles will help me build better data visualizations and user interfaces, and will enable me to clearly convey design concepts to developers and other stakeholders.
